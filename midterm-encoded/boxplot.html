Response Times by Call Type

This boxplot visualizes average response time in minutes across multiple incident types.

Learn More

SFFD Average Response Times by Call Type in Dec 2016

This boxplot provides us with lots of information regarding the time it takes for the SFFD to respond to various incident types. Each incident is grouped into one of the eight call categories. Box-and-whisker plots prove useful in allowing the viewer to observe the distribution of each response per call. Each box has a lower and upper whisker, providing the minimum and maximum. The line outlining the bottom of each box is the first quartile, Q1. The line following that is our median, splitting the data evenly in half and depicts the second quartile, Q2. The line outlining the top of the box is the third quartile, Q3. Note that the plot divides the data into 4 equal parts. The bottom whisker represents the bottom 25% of the data, the bottom half of the box represents the second 25%, the upper half of the box represents the third 25% , and the top whisker represents the top 25%. Each yellow dot above or below the graph represents an outlier, defined to be Q1 - (1.5 * interquartile range) or Q3 + (1.5 * interquartile range) where the interquartile range is defined to be Q3 - Q1. This is the pink box we see enclosing the 3 quartiles.

Enough explaining box plots! The meat of this visualization is in seeing where the SFFD responds the fastest and slowest. Response time is defined by the difference in time between when the call was first dispatched and when SFFD units were on-scene. The fastest response time, on average, seems to be around 4 minutes for alarm incidents. Coming very close to that are fire incidents, at around 4.2 minutes average response. That's pretty good in comparison to a whopping 7.5 minutes for the average medical response. Almost double the time, we would assume on the contrary that medical incidents would be responded to at faster rates. Also note the variance in the interquartile range! There's a pretty wide range of response time for rescue calls in comparison to the much smaller variation in alarm or fire calls. We can therefore be confident within this month that such calls would be responded to, on average, faster than most calls! This can lead us to naturally wonder if our data can be generalized to all months or only specific to December. It would also prove useful to also know the details of calls pertaining 'Other' and 'Alarms' in order to better interpret the visualization.
